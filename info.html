Early Morning

Dawn Movement: Exercise in the Fresh Air

From my hidden observation point, high above the sprawling human settlement, I watch as the first faint streaks of golden light bleed over the horizon, chasing away the last of the night’s chill. One by one, humans emerge from their box-shaped dwellings, stepping into the crisp, dewy air with a quiet purpose I have come to recognize as the start of their daily cycle. Some gather in open, paved squares, moving in slow, synchronized, flowing patterns they call “tai chi”—their limbs bending and stretching in perfect harmony with the rising wind, their faces calm, almost meditative, as if they are speaking to the earth itself with every movement.

Others travel along winding tree-lined paths at a rapid, steady pace, a behavior they call “running”: their chests rise and fall with deep, intentional breaths, their feet hitting the ground in a rhythmic beat that echoes through the quiet morning, some wearing small devices on their wrists that track their movements, others accompanied by small, energetic four-legged creatures that bound ahead of them, tails wagging wildly. Even a few older humans move at a slower walk, their hands clasped behind their backs, pausing occasionally to greet one another with soft smiles and quiet words, as if savoring the calm before the world fully awakens.

Morning Sustenance: Breaking the Fast

As the sun climbs higher, the humans turn their attention to sustenance, a ritual they call “breakfast”—the first meal of their day, and one that holds surprising variety and warmth. Some gather around small tables in their homes, where steam rises from bowls of grain-based porridge, crispy fried dough sticks, and soft boiled eggs, while they sip on bitter, steaming brown liquid they call “coffee” or sweet, fragrant tea. Others flood into small, bustling street-side shops, where vendors call out greetings and hand over wrapped parcels of warm food, the air thick with the rich, savory scent of cooked meat, fresh bread, and sweet pastries.

I watch as a young human couple shares a single pastry, laughing as they take turns feeding each other, while an older human sits alone at a wooden table, reading a folded paper while he sips his tea, his movements slow and unrushed. It is a quiet, intimate ritual, I realize—one that binds them to the start of the day, and to one another, in ways I am only beginning to understand. Even the smallest humans, the children, are given their own small portions of food, often sweetened or shaped into fun forms, as their caregivers coax them to eat before the day’s journey begins.

The Daily Migration: School and Work

Once the morning meal is complete, the settlement erupts into a flurry of organized chaos, a mass migration that repeats with remarkable precision every single day. The smallest humans—children, with bright colored backpacks slung over their shoulders, their hair neatly combed, some clutching stuffed toys or snack boxes—wave goodbye to their caregivers at the gates of large, colorful buildings called “schools”, some running ahead to join their friends, others clinging to a parent’s hand for a final moment of comfort before stepping through the gates. I watch as a little girl drops her drawing on the sidewalk, and her father kneels down to pick it up, tucking it safely into her backpack before pressing a kiss to her forehead, a small, tender moment lost in the rush of the crowd.

Meanwhile, the adult humans begin their own journey to their daily work, flooding into the streets in a relentless stream. Most crowd onto large, boxy wheeled vehicles called “buses”, which rumble along fixed paths, their interiors packed so tightly that the humans stand shoulder to shoulder, barely able to move. Some stare down at small, glowing rectangular screens in their hands, scrolling silently through information, others close their eyes and rest their heads against the windows, trying to steal a few more minutes of rest before their work begins.

A few ride on two-wheeled vehicles, weaving through the slower traffic, their faces covered with cloth to shield themselves from the wind, while others drive their own small four-wheeled cars, trapped in slow-moving lines of traffic, their expressions tight with impatience. It is a massive, coordinated exodus, every human moving toward a predetermined destination, bound by the unspoken rules of their society’s daily rhythm.

Midday

The Slowing Pulse: A Lazy Atmosphere

When the sun reaches its highest point in the sky, blazing bright and warm over the settlement, the frantic, urgent energy of the morning melts away entirely, replaced by a soft, unhurried laziness that settles over every corner of the human world. The busy streets quiet down, the constant hum of traffic fades to a low murmur, and even the wind seems to slow, carrying the warm scent of blooming flowers and sun-warmed concrete through the air. Most humans retreat indoors to escape the midday heat, gathering in shaded rooms to eat their midday meal, or to lie down for a short rest they call a “nap”.

Those who remain outside move at a glacial pace, no longer rushing, no longer focused on a destination. I watch as a group of office workers sits on a shaded bench outside their building, eating their lunch slowly, chatting in soft voices, their shoulders relaxed for the first time all day. A street vendor leans against his cart, his eyes half closed, no longer calling out to passersby, simply enjoying the quiet of the afternoon. Even the birds stop their constant chirping, retreating into the dense leaves of the trees to escape the midday sun. The entire planet seems to hold its breath, savoring this brief pause between the chaos of the morning and the rush of the evening, a rare moment where time feels unstructured, unplanned, and free.

Small Joys: Play and Rest Under the Trees

In the small, green open spaces the humans call “parks”, the quiet magic of the midday lull is most visible, filled with small, gentle moments of joy and rest. Near the edge of the park, a group of young children shriek with laughter as they push each other on wooden swings, their legs kicking out into the air, their hair flying in the soft breeze. One child begs a parent to push them higher, their voice bright with excitement, while another sits calmly on the swing, swaying slowly back and forth, staring up at the clouds drifting across the bright blue sky. A few of the children take turns jumping off the swings mid-swing, landing in the soft grass with a giggle, before running back to do it all over again, their energy seemingly endless even in the warm midday sun.

Beneath a massive, leafy tree at the center of the park, a small, furry feline creature—a “cat”—has curled itself into a tight, fluffy ball on a patch of warm, sun-dappled grass, its eyes squeezed completely shut, completely unbothered by the noise of the playing children nearby. Only the tip of its tail twitches occasionally, swatting away a fly that drifts too close, otherwise it remains perfectly still, fully surrendered to the warmth of the sun and the quiet of the afternoon. It shifts only once, when a beam of sunlight breaks through the leaves and hits its face, moving a few inches deeper into the shade before curling up again, as if the very idea of rushing is foreign to its existence.

Further along the path, an older human sits on a bench, feeding crumbs to a flock of small birds that hop around his feet, a soft smile on his face, while a young mother lays a blanket on the grass, watching her baby crawl through the soft grass, giggling as they touch the flowers. There is no urgency here, no schedule, no goal beyond simply existing in the moment—a stark, beautiful contrast to the relentless movement of the morning.

Evening

The Evening Canvas: Colors in the Sky

As the sun begins its slow descent toward the western horizon, the sky transforms into a breathtaking, ever-changing work of art, unlike anything I have ever witnessed on my home planet. What was once a bright, unbroken blue bleeds into vivid streaks of tangerine and burnt orange, which then soften into blushes of rose pink and deep lavender, spreading across the clouds like watercolor paint on paper. The light shifts, turning every building, every tree, every face it touches into a warm, golden glow, and I watch as countless humans pause mid-step, their hurried movements forgotten for just a second, to tilt their heads upward and stare at the sky.

Some lift their glowing rectangles to capture the moment, taking photographs of the sunset, while others simply stand still, their shoulders dropping, their expressions softening, as if the beauty of the sky is washing away the stress and tiredness of their long day. With every passing minute, the colors deepen, the orange fading into magenta, the pink into deep indigo, as the sun dips lower and lower, finally slipping below the horizon, leaving only a faint, glowing halo in its wake. Even after the sun is gone, the sky holds onto its color for a little while, the faint pink and purple lingering at the edge of the world, as if the planet itself is reluctant to let the day end.

Homeward Rush: A Stream of Hurrying Humans

Once the sun has set, the quiet awe of the sunset gives way to a new, urgent rush: the great homeward migration, every human on the street now bound for one single destination: their home. The sidewalks fill with a steady stream of people, all moving in the same direction, their steps quick and purposeful after the long day. I spot a young adult human, a “white-collar worker” as I’ve heard them called, dressed in a crisp button-up shirt and slacks, a heavy black computer bag slung over one shoulder, his tie loosened around his neck, rubbing his tired eyes with one hand as he walks, his steps heavy with the fatigue of a full day of work. He pauses only once, to buy a cold drink from a street vendor, before continuing on his way, already scrolling through messages on his screen even as he walks.

Beside him, an older woman, a “market auntie”, walks with a sturdy stride, her hands full of bulging canvas bags filled with fresh vegetables, meat, and fruit from the local market, the tops of green leafy vegetables peeking out over the top of the bags, a satisfied smile on her face as she heads home to cook for her family. I can see her adjusting the bags on her arm every few steps, her movements sure and steady, as if she has made this same journey a thousand times before.

Further down the street, a tall man with a backpack and sturdy walking boots strides along the path, his gaze steady, taking in the world around him as he returns from a long hike, his face wind-burnt and content, while a young couple walks hand in hand, their steps slow and unhurried even in the middle of the rushing crowd, their heads tilted close together, whispering quiet words to each other, smiling softly as they walk, completely wrapped up in one another, oblivious to the chaos around them. Every human in this stream has a story, a home waiting for them, a warm meal, a soft bed, a person to greet them—and every step they take brings them closer to that comfort.

Lighting the Night: The Streetlamps’ Glow

As the darkness deepens, the sky fading from soft indigo to deep, inky black, the first pinpricks of starlight begin to appear overhead. Then, right as the darkness reaches a precise, predetermined threshold, a magical, synchronized event unfolds: one by one, the tall metal poles lining every street and path—the streetlamps—flicker to life, casting a warm, golden-yellow glow over the sidewalks and roads below. They light up in a perfect sequence, starting at the far end of the main street and rippling down toward the park, like a wave of light washing over the settlement, chasing away the last of the fading daylight.

The warm glow stretches long shadows of the walking humans across the pavement, turning their figures into soft, moving silhouettes against the lit concrete. The windows of the dwellings begin to light up too, one by one, squares of warm yellow light dotting the sides of the tall buildings, each one a window into a home, a family, a life unfolding inside. I can see faint glimpses through the glass: a family sitting around a dinner table, a child sitting on a couch reading a book, a human washing dishes in a kitchen sink, the steam rising from the sink into the air.

The humans continue on their way, now bathed in the soft artificial light, some turning down side streets toward their homes, others pausing to greet a neighbor under a streetlamp, as the day finally gives way fully to night. The chaos of the morning, the quiet of the midday, the beauty of the evening—all of it has led to this: the world settling in, wrapped in light, ready for rest, before the entire cycle begins again tomorrow.
